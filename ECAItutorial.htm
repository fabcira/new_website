Short Description  

The aim of the tutorial is to provide an insight into Web-based Adaptive Information Extraction from texts from the point of view of both research and applications.  We intend to discuss issues related to designing, implementing and using an adaptive IE system. We believe that these issues are fundamental in understanding the potential and limitations of current technology both in research and application terms. The tutorial is designed for researchers, students and practitioners in the area of IE as well as in related areas such as information retrieval, data integration, natural language processing, machine learning and text mining. the potentialations The audience is expected to have very limited knowledge of IE.

Background

Nowadays, most knowledge is stored in an unstructured textual format. We can’t query it in simple ways, thus automatic systems can’t use mani upulate, and humans can’t easily manage, the contained knowledge and humans can’t easily manage it. Shallow information retrieval tools such as search engines can help users find documents that are broadly relevant.   However, such approaches do not enable fine-grained complex queries, such as finding specific document fragments (“1676.79”) that answer detailed questions (“At what value did NASDAQ close today?”).   Many fields fields (e.g. Knowledge Management) deeply suffer from the bottleneck of acquiring knowledge from texts.

In this context, information extraction (IE) from texts is one of the most promising areas of human language technology. IE is an automatic method for locating important facts in electronic documents—for example, information highlighting for enriching a document or storing information for further use ( such as e.g.  populating an ontology or a data base with instances ) ).

Information extraction has a long history, originating in the natural language processing community (cf r. the MUC conferences).  Most of the work in the past referred to IE from newspaper-like texts. This is a serious limitation for portability, especially for areas such as Knowledge Management, where an increase in the use of Inter- and intranet technologies has moved the focus from free-texts-only scenarios (based on, for example, reports and protocols) to more composite scenarios including semi- and structured texts such as Web pages, emails and Usenet posts. Linguistically intensive approaches as used in classical IE systems are difficult or unnecessary in such cases [Ciravegna 2001b].

Moreover the main barrier to the use of IE in real world applications is the difficulty in adapting IE systems to new scenarios and tasks, as most of the current technology still requires the intervention of IE experts. This makes IE a technology difficult to apply, because personnel skilled in IE are difficult to find in industry, especially in small medium enterprises [Ciravegna 2000]. One of the main current research goals for IE is developing methodologies to enable people with no or scarce preparation in IE to adapt IE systems. This is particularly important for fields such as Knowledge Management where IE is just one of the many technologies to be used in building complex applications: wider acceptance of IE will come only when IE tools will not require any specific skill apart from notions of KM. The potential market for such tools is quite large.

In the last years a new research focus on adaptive IE has arisen at the convergence of natural language processing, Web information integration, and machine learning. The goal of this work is to produce IE algorithms and systems that can be rapidly adapted to new Web-based domains and scenarios.   Some of this work (e.g. [Kushmerick 97, 00; Muslea 98]) focuses on “wrapper induction”, where the documents to be extracted are highly regular, such as HTML text emitted by CGI scripts.   Adaptive IE algorithms have also been developed for the sort of natural text that original ly motivated IE research, such as newspaper articles and email messages (e.g. [Califf 98; Freitag 99; Freitag 00]).   Finally, hybrid approaches have been developed that promise accurate extraction from both regular HTML and natural texts (e.g., [Soderland 99; Freitag & Kushmerick 00; Ciravegna 01]).

Adaptive Web-based IE has been shown to be feasible and successful both from a scientific and application point of view.  As a result, a variety of real-world applications have started to appear, ranging from knowledge Knowledge M management to e-business, and the application of IE to the Internet is gaining increasing importance from an economic point of view.
